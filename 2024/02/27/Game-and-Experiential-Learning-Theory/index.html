Game and Experiential Learning Theory in Game Design

Author: David Kolb

Description:

David Kolb’s Experiential Learning Theory has been widely applied to various fields, including game design. This theory suggests that learning is a continuous process that involves concrete experience, reflective observation, abstract conceptualization, and active experimentation. When applied to game design, this theory emphasizes the importance of providing players with engaging and meaningful experiences that allow for learning and growth.

Conclusion of this theory:

In game design, Kolb’s theory highlights the significance of creating games that offer players the opportunity to actively engage in the learning process through gameplay. By providing hands-on experiences, encouraging reflection, and incorporating opportunities for experimentation, game designers can enhance the overall learning experience for players.

Implications to RPG designers:

  1. Character Development: Incorporating elements that allow players to create and develop their characters throughout the game can promote experiential learning. Games like The Witcher 3: Wild Hunt and Mass Effect showcase this concept by allowing players to make decisions that impact their character’s progression and the game’s narrative.

  2. Quest Design: Designing quests that require players to solve problems, make decisions, and navigate challenges can facilitate experiential learning. Games like The Legend of Zelda: Breath of the Wild and The Elder Scrolls V: Skyrim offer players diverse quests that encourage critical thinking and decision-making.

  3. Interactive Environments: Creating interactive environments that respond to players’ actions and decisions can enhance the learning experience. Games like Red Dead Redemption 2 and Horizon Zero Dawn feature dynamic environments that adapt to players’ choices, fostering a sense of immersion and exploration.

  4. Narrative Engagement: Crafting compelling narratives that immerse players in the game world can facilitate experiential learning. Games like The Last of Us and Bioshock Infinite excel in storytelling, drawing players into the narrative and evoking emotional responses that enhance the overall gameplay experience.

By applying Kolb’s Experiential Learning Theory to RPG design, game developers can create engaging and impactful experiences that promote learning, skill development, and player growth. Incorporating elements such as character development, quest design, interactive environments, and narrative engagement can enrich the overall gaming experience and create lasting impressions on players.

Remember, the key to successful RPG design lies in providing players with meaningful and immersive experiences that encourage active participation and facilitate experiential learning.
